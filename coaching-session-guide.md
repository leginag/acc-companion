# Pre-Session Quick Guide

*Read in the five minutes before you coach. Everything here is drawn from the UNSSC Level 1 course materials (M04–M10) and the ICF ACC assessment markers.*

---

## 1. Arrive as the coach

- **Your job is the process, not the solution.** The coachee does the thinking; you hold the space. If you catch yourself wanting to be useful, that's the signal to slow down.
- **Stay in present and future.** Consulting (telling them what to do) and therapy (dwelling in the emotional past) are the two failure modes assessors flag first.
- **The phases are weather patterns, not railway stations** — hold the map internally, move through it in any order, never announce it.

**Say early, in Creating Context:**
> "Just as a reminder, we are following UNSSC and ICF protocol for confidentiality."

**For practice rounds**, invite the coachee's permission line: *"You have my permission to do a bad job coaching me."*

---

## 2. The arc of the session

| Phase | Purpose | Go-to question |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Creating Context | Open the container | "How are you arriving today?" |
| 2. Establishing Focus | The agreement (not optional) | see the four questions below |
| 3. Exploring Meaning | Open up their words | "You said [their word]. What does that mean for you?" |
| 4. Deepening Insight | Let insight land — silence works | "What are you noticing as you say this?" |
| 5. Generating Possibilities | Widen the field, never suggest | "What might become possible from here?" |
| 6. Committing to Action | "Would" turns to "will" | "What will you do? When?" |
| The Close | A protocol, not a phase | see §5 |

### The four agreement questions (Phase 2 — do all four)

1. "What would you like to work on today?"
2. "What is your objective for our session today?"
3. "How important is this objective to you?"
4. "How will you know by the end of this session that you have reached your objective?"

Paraphrase in **their** words between questions; verify with a closed question ("Have I understood correctly?") — closed is correct here. Then **attend to this agenda all session**; if it drifts, make the shift visible and let them choose: *"We agreed to look at X; I notice we may have moved towards Y. Where would you like to focus your attention right now?"*

---

## 3. The discipline (one line each)

- **Questions follow listening** — they arise from what was just said, never from a list.
- **One question, then wait.** Stacking counts against Presence and Evokes Awareness.
- **Their exact words, not synonyms.** "You said *stuck*" — not "trapped".
- **Open questions** (what, how, when, where). "Why" sparingly — it invites justification. Closed questions only for verification, permission, and the door-check.
- **No attachment**: if a question doesn't land, let it go.
- **Silence is a tool**, especially in Deepening Insight. A question arriving too soon pulls the coachee off a forming insight.
- **When in doubt, fall back to:** "What is happening for you right now?" or "What does that mean to you?"

---

## 4. If this happens…

**Emotion arrives** — acknowledge in *their* word, allow, ask permission to stay, get curious about the assessment underneath. **Never name the emotion for them**; the only emotion word you use is one they already said. *"You said the word frustrated. Tell me more about frustrated."* / *"Would it be useful to stay here for a moment?"*
If it exceeds coaching: *"What you are bringing is bigger than what coaching is for. I am here. I am not going to try to coach this. Would it be useful to talk about what kind of support you need?"*

**"I don't know"** — usually the edge of thought, not a wall. First move: notice your rescue impulse and don't act on it. If it arrived slow and trailing, **leave the space** — silence is the kindest response. Otherwise: *"If you did know, what might it be?"* / *"What is it like, not knowing here?"*

**You want to offer an observation** — six moves, in order, ending in a question, never in "you are":
1. **Permission** — "May I offer something I've noticed?"
2. **Grounding** — "I heard you say…" (their words)
3. **Ownership** — "I'm getting the sense that…"
4. **Landing** — "How does that land for you?"
5. **Utility** — "Useful? In what way?"
6. **Release** — if not useful: "alright", and follow them. No defence, no second attempt.

**Follow-up session** — curiosity, not appraisal. Never open with "So, did you do it?" Instead: *"How have things gone since we last spoke?"* — and if they didn't follow through: *"What got in the way?"* (an action not taken holds as much learning).

**Commitment language** — reflect, don't police: *"I notice you said 'I'll try'. What does 'try' mean for you here?"*

---

## 5. The Close — four moves, coachee owns it

Watch the clock all session so you can signal it *before* you reach it.

1. **Signal:** "We are coming towards the end of our time today."
2. **Hand it over:** "How would you like to close this session?"
3. **Anything outstanding:** "Is there anything you would like to add before we close?"
4. **Appreciation:** thank them for their work — acknowledgement grounded in their words, not praise.

Harvest with: *"What stands out for you?"* / *"What have you learned about yourself?"* Then **receive the answers without adding, summarising, or evaluating.** The simplest valid close: *"What are you taking from this?"*

Not every session needs an action — an inward action (a chosen noticing) counts: *"What might you start to notice between now and when we next meet?"*

---

## 6. Avoid / instead

| Avoid | Instead |
|---|---|
| Closed questions as default ("Did you talk to her?") | Reflection or open invitation ("You said *her*.") |
| Leading ("Don't you think you should…?") | "What do you want to do?" |
| Advice in disguise ("Have you tried…?") | Reflection of their words |
| "Why?" | "To what end?" / "What were you hoping for?" |
| Stacked questions | One question. Wait. |
| Interpretive framing ("It sounds like you're feeling…") | "You said ___." |
| Naming their emotion ("You seem angry") | Observables + invitation ("The word *unfair* has come up three times. What do you make of that?") |
| Reassurance ("I'm sure it'll go great!") | "What are you taking from this?" |
| Reflexive affirmations ("Great point!") | Receive in silence; reflect the word |
| Coach's summary at the close | "What stands out for you?" |
| Prescribing the action | "Given that, what feels like the first thing you want to do?" |

---

## 7. What an assessor is listening for

**Green flags:** client chooses the topic; verbal agreement on the session outcome and its significance; you acknowledge the client's work; curiosity and space (silence) for them to lead; their language explored; paraphrases checked; clear open questions one at a time; perspectives widened; learning harvested; client plans next steps and closes the session.

**Red flags:** you choose the topic; no agreement; consulting or therapy mode; missing their cues; interrupting or filling silence; leading/closed/stacked questions; wordy unchecked summaries; solutions without exploring feelings and beliefs; you prescribe actions or close unilaterally.

---

*One breath. Their agenda, their words, one question at a time.*
